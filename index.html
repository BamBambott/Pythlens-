// Price Feeds
01
Pull Oracle Model
Unlike push oracles that broadcast prices to all chains constantly, Pyth uses a pull model — apps fetch signed price updates from Hermes on demand. This reduces gas waste and keeps data fresh.
02
Confidence Intervals
Every Pyth price comes with a ±confidence value representing uncertainty. A BTC price of $84,000 ±$12 means high conviction. A wide band like ±$800 signals market stress or low liquidity.
03
First-Party Data
Pyth aggregates from 120+ institutional publishers — exchanges, market makers, prop trading firms — not derived from secondary sources. Each submits a price and their own confidence estimate.
04
Exponent Encoding
Prices are stored as integers with a negative exponent. A raw price of 8400000000000 with expo -8 means $84,000.00. This allows fixed-point math without floating-point imprecision on-chain.

// What is a Confidence Interval?
Pyth publishes a confidence interval alongside every price. This ± value reflects how much disagreement exists between data providers at that moment.

A narrow band means high conviction — all publishers agree. A wide band signals stress: extreme volatility, exchange outages, or liquidity divergence.

The stress score = confidence / price × 10,000 bps. Above 10bps = caution. Above 50bps = high stress.
